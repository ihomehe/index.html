User Guide · Airtable

How to manage a submission workflow in Airtable

A guide for editorial teams tracking high-volume submissions

AudienceEditorial coordinators and managing editors
ContextUsed at PRISM International for tracking 4,000+ annual submissions
Time15 minutes to set up
Overview

Airtable lets your editorial team track submissions, assign reviewers, and monitor status in one shared workspace. This guide walks through setting up a basic submission tracker from scratch.

Before you begin

You will need an Airtable account (free tier is sufficient for most editorial teams) and editor-level access to your team's workspace. Ask your workspace admin to invite you before starting.

Steps
  1. Create a new base

    From your Airtable home screen, click + Create and select Start from scratch. Name your base clearly — for example, "PRISM Submissions 2026." This becomes your team's shared workspace for the submission cycle.

  2. Set up your fields

    Your base opens with a default table. Rename it "Submissions" and add the following fields: Submitter Name (Single line text), Genre (Single select), Date Received (Date), Assigned Reviewer (Collaborator), Status (Single select: Unread, In Review, Accepted, Declined), Notes (Long text).

  3. Configure your Status field options

    Click the Status field header and select Customize field. Add your status options and assign a colour to each — for example, grey for Unread, yellow for In Review, green for Accepted, red for Declined. Colour coding makes it easy to read submission status at a glance.

  4. Create a Kanban view for reviewers

    Click + Add view in the left panel and select Kanban. Set the grouping field to Status. Reviewers can now drag submissions between columns as they move through the review process, without needing to edit records directly.

  5. Share with your team

    Click Share in the top right corner. Invite team members by email and set their permission level. Assign Editor access to reviewers so they can update submission status. Assign Commenter access to readers who only need to view the tracker.

  6. Filter by reviewer to manage assignments

    Use the Filter button in the toolbar to filter by Assigned Reviewer. Each team member can apply this filter to see only their assigned submissions, reducing confusion in high-volume cycles.

Note If your team receives submissions via email or a form, Airtable's form view can capture submissions directly into the base. Go to + Add view and select Form to set this up.
Important Free Airtable workspaces have a 1,000-record limit per base. If your team processes more than 1,000 submissions per cycle, consider archiving closed records to a separate base at the end of each season, or upgrading to a paid plan.

User Guide · Canvas LMS

Getting started with Canvas: setting up your first course

A guide for new instructors building a course from scratch

AudienceNew instructors and teaching assistants
ContextUsed at UBC School of Creative Writing for onboarding teaching staff
Time20–30 minutes for initial setup
Overview

Canvas organizes course content into modules — folders that group related material and guide students through your course in a clear sequence. This guide walks through building your first module, adding your syllabus, and publishing your course so students can access it.

Before you begin

You will need an active Canvas account with instructor-level access to your course shell. If your course shell has not been created yet, contact your department administrator. Your course will remain invisible to students until you publish it.

Steps
  1. Open your course

    Log in to Canvas and select your course from the Dashboard. If you do not see your course, click All Courses in the left navigation panel. Unpublished courses appear with a grey dot next to the course name.

  2. Navigate to Modules

    In the left sidebar, click Modules. This is where you will organize all course content. A blank course will show no modules — this is expected.

  3. Create your first module

    Click + Module in the top right corner. Name your first module clearly — "Start Here" or "Week 1: Introduction" both work well. A clear module name helps students understand where to begin without needing to ask.

  4. Add your syllabus to the module

    Click the + button inside your module and select File. Upload your syllabus document. Once uploaded, it will appear as an item inside the module. Students will be able to download it directly from the course page.

  5. Record and upload a welcome video (recommended)

    A short welcome video — two to three minutes — reduces early student anxiety and sets expectations for the course. Record using Loom or any screen recording tool, then add the link to your module by clicking + and selecting External URL. Label it clearly: "Welcome from your instructor."

  6. Publish the module

    Click the grey circle icon next to your module name to publish it. The icon will turn green. Students cannot see module content until both the module and the course are published.

  7. Publish the course

    Return to your course home page. Click the Publish button in the top right corner of the screen. Your course is now visible to enrolled students.

Note You can add assignments, quizzes, and discussions at any time after your modules are set up. It is good practice to build your module structure first, then add assessment items inside each module so students can see how the course is sequenced.
Important Publishing the course makes it visible to all enrolled students immediately. If your course is not ready for students to view, keep it unpublished until setup is complete.

User Guide · Dropbox

How to organize shared team folders in Dropbox

A guide for editorial and production teams managing shared assets

AudienceTeam leads and editorial coordinators
ContextUsed at PRISM International for shared asset management across print and digital production
Time10 minutes to set up
Overview

Dropbox shared folders let your team access, upload, and edit files from one central location. This guide walks through creating a shared folder structure for an editorial or production team, setting permissions, and keeping your library organized over time.

Before you begin

You will need a Dropbox account with sufficient storage for your team's files. The person creating the shared folder becomes its owner by default and is responsible for managing access. Make sure you have the email addresses of the team members you want to invite.

Steps
  1. Create a top-level team folder

    In Dropbox, click New folder and name it after your team or project — for example, "PRISM 2026." This folder will be the root of your shared file structure. All subfolders and assets will live inside it.

  2. Set up your subfolder structure

    Inside your top-level folder, create subfolders for each major category of content your team works with. For an editorial team, a clear structure might be: Submissions, Design Assets, Issue Archives, Contracts and Admin. Use consistent naming conventions across all subfolders — for example, always use the year prefix: "2026-Issue-04."

  3. Share the folder with your team

    Right-click the top-level folder and select Share. Enter your team members' email addresses and set their permission level. Choose Can edit for team members who upload and manage files. Choose Can view for stakeholders who only need to review assets.

  4. Use consistent file naming

    Establish a naming convention before your team starts uploading. A reliable format is: YYYY-MM-DD_ProjectName_Version — for example, 2026-03-01_CoverDesign_v2.pdf. Consistent naming makes files easier to search and reduces duplicate uploads.

  5. Create a shared link for external collaborators

    If you need to share a specific file or folder with someone outside your team — a printer, a contributor, or a designer — right-click the item and select Copy link. Set the link permission to Can view to prevent unintended edits. Share the link directly rather than inviting them to the folder.

  6. Archive completed projects

    At the end of each production cycle, move completed project folders into an Archive subfolder. This keeps your active workspace uncluttered while preserving access to past assets. Avoid deleting files unless you are certain they are no longer needed — deleted files can be recovered from Dropbox for up to 180 days on most plans.

Note Dropbox syncs automatically across all devices. Team members with the Dropbox desktop app installed will see shared folder updates in real time without needing to refresh or re-download files.
Important Only the folder owner can change permission settings or remove members. If your team lead changes, transfer folder ownership before they leave the organization. Go to the folder's sharing settings and select Transfer ownership.
